Fed Officials Signal Pause on Rate Cuts Amid Sticky Inflation

By Nick Timiraos · Updated April 21, 2026

Federal Reserve officials signaled they are likely to hold interest rates steady at their upcoming meeting, as recent inflation data suggests price pressures are proving more persistent than policymakers had hoped. The shift in tone marks a departure from earlier guidance that had hinted at additional easing later this year.

Speaking at a conference in Chicago on Monday, several regional Fed presidents emphasized that the "last mile" of disinflation — bringing price growth sustainably back to the central bank's 2% target — may require keeping policy restrictive for longer. Markets responded by trimming bets on near-term rate cuts, with the two-year Treasury yield climbing to a four-month high.

The hawkish pivot comes as consumer prices rose 3.1% in March from a year earlier, above economists' forecasts, while the labor market has remained resilient. Fed Chair Jerome Powell is scheduled to deliver a speech on Wednesday that traders will parse for further clues on the central bank's trajectory.

Critics of the Fed's approach argue that maintaining elevated rates risks tipping the economy into recession, particularly as small businesses and lower-income households grapple with higher borrowing costs. Supporters counter that prematurely loosening policy could re-anchor inflation expectations at an unacceptably high level.

Traders in federal-funds futures now price in only one quarter-point cut by year end, down from three as recently as February. Some strategists warn that yield-curve dynamics could become unstable if long-dated Treasury yields keep rising while the front end stays pinned by the Fed's hold.
